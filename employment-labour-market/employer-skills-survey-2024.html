What “skills gap” vs “skill-shortage vacancy” mean. A skill-shortage vacancy (SSV) is a post an employer can’t fill because applicants lack the skills, qualifications or experience — an external recruitment problem. A skills gap is existing staff not being fully proficient — an internal problem. The North East’s overall deficiency rate (16%) is mid-pack, but its skilled-trades gap (4% of all employers) is the highest of any region.

The standout positive. The North East has the highest apprenticeship engagement of any English region — 38% currently offer or have offered apprenticeships in the last three years (England employer-weighted average ~26%), and 17% currently have apprentices on site. Yet training incidence overall (59%) is only mid-table, and 41% of NE employers funded no training at all in the last year.

The digital paradox. The North East has both the lowest current AI use (11%) and the lowest planned adoption among non-users (6%) of any region — yet the few NE employers who have adopted AI are the most committed to embedding it further (92% plan to, the highest of any region). Set against the highest upskilling need in England (68%) and the second-highest rate of over-qualified staff (49%), the picture is of latent capacity and demand that current take-up isn’t meeting.

About this dashboard

Built from the Department for Education’s Employer Skills Survey (ESS) 2024 — England regional data tables, the largest UK survey of employer demand for skills. Fieldwork by IFF Research; the 2024 wave covers tens of thousands of establishment interviews across England, weighted to the full employer population. This dashboard reads the nine English regions and foregrounds the North East.

Why there is no Gateshead data here

This particular workbook is the regional table pack — its smallest geography is the nine Government Office Regions. It contains no local-authority, combined-authority or borough breakdowns, so Gateshead cannot be isolated from it. The North East region (here, 61,239 employers / ~1.08m employees) is the closest available stand-in.

If you need Gateshead specifically, DfE publishes ESS local-authority-district estimates separately on the Explore Education Statistics platform (key measures such as vacancy density, skill-shortage and skills-gap density at LAD level). Those tables drop straight into this same structure when you want a Gateshead build.

How to read it

  • Skill-shortage vacancy (SSV) — a vacancy left hard to fill because applicants lack the right skills, qualifications or experience (an external/recruitment problem).
  • Skills gap — existing staff who are not fully proficient at their job (an internal/workforce problem).
  • Employer base vs employment base — most measures here are the % of employers reporting something. “Staff trained” and “staff with skills gaps” are counts of people.
  • England (weighted) rows/lines are computed in-browser as the employer-count-weighted mean of the nine regions — the pack provides no England total.

Notes & caveats

  • The North East has the smallest unweighted sample of the nine regions (342 employer interviews on the core base; fewer on module questions — e.g. ~84–93 on the apprenticeship and T-Level modules). Treat North East figures, especially module-based ones, as more uncertain, and small differences between regions as indicative rather than precise.
  • Percentages are rounded to whole numbers; ranks and the weighted England average are computed from those rounded values, so ties and ±1 differences should be read loosely.
  • ESS is a point-in-time survey of establishments (sites), not enterprises — a large multi-site employer counts once per site.
  • Statistical-significance flags in the source tables (95% level) informed the “highest/lowest in England” callouts; the underlying tables carry the full significance lettering.
