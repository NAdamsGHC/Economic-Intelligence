How rank, percentile and severity are derived

Rank = MHCLG's official rank of average score local-authority summary (File 10): Gateshead's population-weighted mean LSOA score on each domain, ranked against all 296 English local authorities. This dashboard reproduces the measure from the LSOA file — including MHCLG's convention of weighting the Employment domain by working-age (18–66) population rather than total population.

Percentile = rank ÷ 296 × 100 (lower = more deprived).

Severity bands are this dashboard's own labels, cut at fixed percentiles: Acute = worst 15% of councils, High = 15–33%, Moderate = 33–60%, Relative strength = above the 60th percentile.

Method & sources

Data

  • English Indices of Deprivation 2025 (IoD2025), Ministry of Housing, Communities & Local Government — File 7: All ranks, scores, deciles and population denominators (LSOA level, 2021 boundaries; population denominators are mid-2022). Release page · File 7 (CSV)
  • File 10: Local Authority District Summaries (lower-tier), v2 — the official LAD summary measures. File 10 (XLSX). The borough and domain ranks shown here equal its rank of average score measure, reproduced independently from File 7 (verified 2026-07). The Overview tab's income and employment headcounts quote its official Income scale and Employment scale directly.
  • Geography: ONS Open Geography Portal — LSOA (2021) → Electoral Ward (2024) → LAD (2024) best-fit lookup, plus 2024 ward and 2021 LSOA generalised boundaries. Wards are Gateshead's current 22 electoral wards.

How figures are derived

Taken directly from the published IoD2025 LSOA file (no calculation): every LSOA's score, national rank and national decile on the overall IMD and each domain, plus the population denominators. Deciles are assigned nationally by MHCLG — decile 1 means that LSOA is in the most deprived 10% of England's 33,755 LSOAs. The deprivation rates used in the headcounts are also quoted, not derived: for Income, Employment, IDACI and IDAOPI the published score is itself the rate — File 7's column headings read "Income Score (rate)" etc. The other five domain scores (Education, Health, Crime, Barriers, Living Environment) are constructed indices, not rates, and are never presented as rates here.

Author's calculations from those published values (each is shown with its arithmetic in the "how calculated" expanders on the relevant tab):

  • Ward IMD scores = Σ(LSOA score × LSOA mid-2022 total population) ÷ ward population, over each ward's constituent LSOAs. Ward ranks 1–22 order the wards by that score — a borough-internal ranking, not a national statistic.
  • Borough and domain LAD ranks = the same population-weighted mean computed for every English local authority from the LSOA file, then ranked (1 = most deprived of 296). This equals MHCLG's official rank of average score summary in File 10 for the overall IMD and all seven domains (the Employment domain is weighted by working-age 18–66 population, following MHCLG's method).
  • Indicator headcounts: the borough income and employment counts are quoted from File 10's official scale measures. Everything else (IDACI, IDAOPI, and all ward-level counts) = Σ(LSOA rate × matched mid-2022 denominator): income → total population; employment → working-age 18–66; children in poverty (IDACI) → children 0–15; older income-deprived (IDAOPI) → residents 60+. These reproduce the official scales within ~0.1–0.3% (the published scores are rounded and shrinkage-adjusted); all are modelled estimates for targeting, not claimant counts.
  • England comparator rates = national Σ(rate × denominator) ÷ national Σ(denominator) across all 33,755 LSOAs.
  • LSOAs are assigned to wards on a best-fit basis (ONS lookup), so ward boundaries are approximate at the edges.

Important caveat — comparability with 2019

IoD2025's Income and Employment domains use Universal Credit data, which captures a broader population (including many in-work, low-income households) than the legacy benefits behind IoD2019. As a result the absolute income/employment rates here are markedly higher than 2019 and should not be read as a change over time. The Indices are designed for relative comparison — ranks and deciles — which is precisely how this dashboard uses them. The overall IMD rank, domain ranks and ward ordering are all relative measures and remain robust.
